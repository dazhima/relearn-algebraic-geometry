# Spec ℤ[x] as a Fibration over Spec ℤ

## The Setup

The inclusion of rings ℤ ↪ ℤ[x] induces a morphism of schemes

$$\pi : \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Z}[x] \longrightarrow \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Z}.$$

This map makes Spec ℤ[x] into a *family of curves parametrized by* Spec ℤ. The idea is to understand each prime ideal 𝔭 ∈ Spec ℤ[x] by asking: *which point of* Spec ℤ *does it lie over?* That is, what is 𝔭 ∩ ℤ?

There are two possibilities: 𝔭 ∩ ℤ = (0) (the generic point of Spec ℤ), or 𝔭 ∩ ℤ = (p) for a prime number p (a closed point of Spec ℤ). This dichotomy is exactly the case split in the proof of Proposition 1.1, and it has a clean geometric meaning in terms of fiber products.



---

## The Fiber Product Picture

For any point $s \in \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Z}$, the **fiber** of $\pi$ over $s$ is defined as the fiber product

$$\pi^{-1}(s) = \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Z}[x] \times_{\operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Z}} \operatorname{Spec} \kappa(s),$$

where $\kappa(s)$ is the **residue field** at $s$. On the algebra side, this is

$$\operatorname{Spec}\!\left(\mathbb{Z}[x] \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \kappa(s)\right).$$

Spec ℤ has two kinds of points, and the two fibers are:

| Point $s \in \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Z}$ | Residue field $\kappa(s)$ | Fiber $\mathbb{Z}[x] \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \kappa(s)$ |
|---|---|---|
| Generic point $(0)$ | $\mathbb{Q}$ | $\operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Q}[x]$ |
| Closed point $(p)$ | $\mathbb{F}_p$ | $\operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{F}_p[x]$ |

Each fiber is a copy of the **affine line** over the respective field — $\mathbb{A}^1_\mathbb{Q}$ over the generic point and $\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{F}_p}$ over each prime $p$. So Spec ℤ[x] is a family of affine lines, one per prime plus one generic, all glued together over the base Spec ℤ.

---

## The Four Types of Primes, Explained by the Fibration

Each prime 𝔭 ∈ Spec ℤ[x] sits in exactly one fiber, and within that fiber it is either a **generic point** or a **closed point**. This gives a clean 2×2 classification.

### Over the Generic Point (0) ∈ Spec ℤ: the fiber Spec ℚ[x]

The fiber is $\operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{Q}[x]$, a PID whose primes are well understood:

**Type 1 — 𝔭 = (0).**
The generic point of *all* of Spec ℤ[x]. It lies over the generic point of Spec ℤ, and it is also the generic point of the fiber Spec ℚ[x]. Geometrically it is the "deepest" point, not closed in any fiber and not closed in the total space.

**Type 2 — 𝔭 = (q(x)) for irreducible q(x) ∈ ℤ[x].**
This is a *closed* point of the fiber Spec ℚ[x]. Since ℚ[x] is a PID, every nonzero prime is principal and generated by an irreducible; after clearing denominators and normalizing (content = 1), this irreducible can be chosen in ℤ[x]. So the closed points of the generic fiber are in bijection with primitive irreducible polynomials in ℤ[x] up to sign. These primes are **not closed** in Spec ℤ[x] itself — their closure also contains all the primes (p, q(x)) lying over primes p dividing the discriminant or values of q.

### Over a Closed Point (p) ∈ Spec ℤ: the fiber Spec 𝔽_p[x]

The fiber is $\operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{F}_p[x]$, again a PID:

**Type 3 — 𝔭 = (p).**
The generic point of the fiber Spec 𝔽_p[x]. Contracting to ℤ gives (p), so this prime lies over (p) ∈ Spec ℤ. It is closed in Spec ℤ but not in its own fiber Spec 𝔽_p[x]. Geometrically, it is the "whole geometric fiber over p," before we pick any specific point on it.

**Type 4 — 𝔭 = (p, q(x)) for irreducible q(x) mod p.**
A *closed* point of the fiber Spec 𝔽_p[x]. These are the genuine closed points of Spec ℤ[x]: they are closed in both the fiber and the total space. The residue field is 𝔽_p[x]/(q(x)) = 𝔽_{p^n}, where n = deg q. The case n = 1 (linear q mod p) gives residue field 𝔽_p itself.

## A Picture of the Fibration

```
Spec ℤ[x]
│
│  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  (closed pts of fiber: Type 4)
│──────────────────── (generic of fiber over p: Type 3)
│
│  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  (closed pts of generic fiber: Type 2)
│──────────────────── (generic of generic fiber: Type 1)
│
└──────────────────── Spec ℤ
        (0)    (2)  (3)  (5)  (7) …
```

Each horizontal line is a copy of Spec k[x] — an affine line over a field. The vertical direction is the map π. Closed points (Type 2 and 4) are the "ordinary" geometric points, while Types 1 and 3 are generic points of their respective strata.

---

## The Proof of Proposition 1.1 as a Fiber-by-Fiber Argument

The proof in the notes is exactly a fiber-by-fiber analysis:

- **Case 1 (𝔭 ∩ ℤ = (0)):** base-change to the generic fiber ℚ[x] by localizing at ℤ \ {0}. Every nonzero prime of ℚ[x] is principal (q(x)), and the prime ideal of ℤ[x] is the contraction — giving Types 1 and 2.

- **Case 2 (𝔭 ∩ ℤ = (p)):** base-change to the fiber 𝔽_p[x] by the quotient ℤ[x] ↠ ℤ[x]/(p) ≅ 𝔽_p[x]. Every prime of 𝔽_p[x] is (0) or principal (q(x)), and contraction back gives Types 3 and 4.

The correspondence theorem (ideal correspondence under surjection) is precisely the statement that the primes of the fiber are in bijection with the primes of ℤ[x] lying over the chosen base point — which is the universal property of the fiber product.

## Why This Matters

The fiber product perspective explains not just the *classification* but the *geometry*:

- **Specialization.** A Type 2 prime (q(x)) specializes to all Type 4 primes (p, q̄(x)) lying over primes p where q̄(x) = q(x) mod p is still irreducible. This is the algebro-geometric shadow of the prime factorization of q(x) mod p.

- **Base change.** Studying a Diophantine equation f(x) = 0 over ℤ becomes, fiber by fiber, studying f over ℚ (the generic fiber) and over 𝔽_p for each prime p (the special fibers). The fiber product formalizes what it means to "reduce mod p."

- **Ramification.** Primes (p, q(x)) where q(x) mod p has repeated roots correspond to *ramification* in the fiber over p — a phenomenon invisible at the level of types alone, but central in algebraic number theory.

Spec ℤ[x] is thus the universal example of an arithmetic surface: a two-dimensional scheme whose horizontal direction is "polynomial" and whose vertical direction is "arithmetic."
