## 1. Uniqueness of Initial and Terminal Objects

**Lemma 1.1.** *Initial and terminal objects, whenever they exist, are unique up to unique isomorphism, i.e., any two objects that are initial (or terminal) are isomorphic to each other, and this isomorphism itself is unique.*

*Proof.* We show the claim for initial objects. Suppose $P$ and $Q$ are two initial objects in a category $\mathcal{C}$. The idea is to "play the objects against each other."

Since $P$ is initial there is a unique morphism $\phi : P \to Q$. Since $Q$ is initial there is a unique morphism $\psi : Q \to P$. Consider $\phi \circ \psi : Q \to Q$. Since $Q$ is initial there is only one map $Q \to Q$, so $\phi \circ \psi = \mathrm{id}_Q$. Similarly $\psi \circ \phi = \mathrm{id}_P$. Thus $\phi$ and $\psi$ are isomorphisms, and they are unique. $\square$

## 2. Maps Between Categories: Functors

It is conceivable that a category is itself an object of a larger category. Such a notion exists and is called a functor.

**Definition 2.1.** Let $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$ be two categories. A *(covariant) functor* $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ is a rule that assigns:

- to an object $A \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$, an object $F(A) \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{D})$, and
- to a morphism $f : A \to B$ of $\mathcal{C}$, a morphism $F(f) : F(A) \to F(B)$ in $\mathcal{D}$.

[The functor is *contravariant* if it reverses the direction of morphisms: to $f : A \to B$ it assigns $F(f) : F(B) \to F(A)$.] This assignment must satisfy:

1. $F(\mathrm{id}_A) = \mathrm{id}_{F(A)}$, and
2. $F(g \circ f) = F(g) \circ F(f)$ if $F$ is covariant, or $F(g \circ f) = F(f) \circ F(g)$ if $F$ is contravariant.

**Example 2.2.** The *forgetful functor* $U : \mathbf{Groups} \to \mathbf{Sets}$. Here $U(G) = G$ as a set (forgetting the group structure), and $U(f : G \to H) = f : G \to H$ as a map of sets.

**Example 2.3.** The *free functor* $F : \mathbf{Sets} \to \mathbf{Groups}$. Here $F(S)$ is the free group generated by elements of $S$. A map $S \to T$ of sets gives rise to a map $F(S) \to F(T)$ between free groups.

**Example 2.4** *(for those who have taken Math 444).* Consider the category $\mathbf{pt\text{-}Top}$ of pointed topological spaces (objects are pairs $(X, x_0)$). The fundamental group is a functor $\pi_1 : \mathbf{pt\text{-}Top} \to \mathbf{Groups}$ assigning $\pi_1(X, x_0)$ to $(X, x_0)$. A map $f : (X, x_0) \to (Y, y_0)$ induces a homomorphism $\pi_1(X, x_0) \to \pi_1(Y, y_0)$, so $\pi_1$ is a covariant functor.

**Example 2.5.** The functor $F : k\textbf{-Vect} \to k\textbf{-Vect}$ sending a vector space to its dual, $F(V) = \mathrm{Hom}(V, k) = V^*$. This is a *contravariant* functor: a linear transformation $f : V \to W$ gives rise to $F(f) : W^* \to V^*$.

### 3.1. Zorn's Lemma and Maximal Ideals

All rings are commutative with unit. Let $A$ be a ring.

- A proper ideal $\mathfrak{p} \subseteq A$ is *prime* if $A/\mathfrak{p}$ is an integral domain; equivalently, if $xy \in \mathfrak{p}$ and $x \notin \mathfrak{p}$, then $y \in \mathfrak{p}$.
- A proper ideal $\mathfrak{m} \subseteq A$ is *maximal* if $A/\mathfrak{m}$ is a field; equivalently, $\mathfrak{m} \subseteq \mathfrak{a} \Rightarrow \mathfrak{a} = A$ for any ideal $\mathfrak{a}$.

Maximal ideals are prime.

Recall: a *partially ordered set* $S$ is a set with a relation $\leq$ that is reflexive, transitive, and antisymmetric. A subset $T \subseteq S$ is a *chain* if every pair of elements is comparable. An *upper bound* on a chain is an element $x \in S$ with $t \leq x$ for every $t \in T$.

**Axiom (Zorn's Lemma).** *Let $S$ be a non-empty partially ordered set. If every chain of $S$ has an upper bound, then $S$ has at least one maximal element.*

This statement is equivalent to the Axiom of Choice. (See Paul Halmos, *Naive Set Theory*.)

**Theorem 3.1.** *Let $A \neq 0$ be a ring. Then $A$ has at least one maximal ideal.*

*Proof.* Let $\Sigma$ be the set of all proper ideals of $A$, ordered by inclusion. $\Sigma \neq \emptyset$ since $A \neq 0$. Let $T = \{\mathfrak{a}_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a chain in $\Sigma$. Then $\mathfrak{a} := \bigcup_{i \in I} \mathfrak{a}_i$ is an upper bound of $T$ (one verifies it is still a proper ideal). By Zorn's Lemma, $\Sigma$ has a maximal element with respect to inclusion. This is a maximal ideal of $A$. $\square$

**Corollary 3.2.** *Let $\mathfrak{a} \subseteq A$ be a proper ideal. Then $A$ has a maximal ideal containing $\mathfrak{a}$.*

*Proof.* Apply the theorem to the quotient ring $A/\mathfrak{a}$ and use the one-to-one correspondence between ideals of $A/\mathfrak{a}$ and ideals of $A$ containing $\mathfrak{a}$. $\square$

**Remark 3.3.** A ring with exactly one maximal ideal is called a *local ring*, written $(A, \mathfrak{m})$. The field $k := A/\mathfrak{m}$ is the *residue field* of the local ring.

### 3.2. Nilradical and Jacobson Radical

Let $N \subseteq A$ be the set of *nilpotent elements* of $A$:

$$N := \{x \in A : x^n = 0 \text{ for some } n > 0\}.$$

This is an ideal of $A$:
- If $x \in N$ then $ax \in N$ for every $a \in A$ (uses commutativity of $A$).
- If $x^n = y^m = 0$ then $(x + y)^{n+m-1} = 0$, so $x + y \in N$.

We call $N$ the *nilradical* of $A$. The quotient ring $A/N$ has trivial nilradical.

**Example 3.4.** $\mathbb{Z}$ has no nilpotent elements other than $0$, so $N = (0)$. On the other hand, $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ has nilpotent elements. In this case $N$ is the principal ideal generated by the class of $\prod_{p \mid n} p$, where $p$ runs over the prime divisors of $n$.
