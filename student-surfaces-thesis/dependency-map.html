Where the black boxes actually come from

The main stem is the reading path: Riemann–Roch and Riemann–Hurwitz on curves, then straight into surfaces, then back to curves once the machinery is unlocked. Every stem node cites specific results from Chapters II and III — this is the map of those citations, and which ones are safe to leave as black boxes versus which ones aren't.

The one rule that decides skippable vs. nonskippable

A citation is skippable if trusting it without proof still leaves you with the full geometric picture — you can compute, compare, and reason about the object, you just haven't verified why a number or an existence statement is true. A citation is nonskippable if trusting it blindly removes the geometric picture itself — most often, anything that embeds an object into projective space. Black-boxing an embedding doesn't just skip a proof, it skips having any concrete mental picture of the curve or surface at all.

Nonskippable — must actually understand
Very ampleness / embedding criteria, for curves and for surfaces. This is the one category worth stopping for immediately, not logging for later.
Skippable — safe long-term black box
Serre duality, Riemann–Roch for surfaces, Noether's formula, adjunction, cohomology-and-base-change, finiteness, the blow-up construction, Castelnuovo's criterion. All computational or existence machinery — trust the output, keep moving, unlock later at leisure.

Zooming in: the very-ampleness kill list

This is the one node from Stem 1 marked nonskippable — expanded into its own dependency chain, so the reading plan for Chapter II is a specific list of items, not "read II.7." The punchline: most of what makes very ampleness feel hard is actually Stem 1's Riemann–Roch, already unlocked. The genuinely new material is four items.

The actual reading assignment Four items, not a new theory: linear systems and their associated morphisms, base-point-freeness, the separates-points/separates-tangent-vectors criterion, and what a closed immersion of schemes is. Once those are genuinely understood, checking that deg D ≥ 2g+1 works is two lines of arithmetic you've already got from Stem 1 — the whole right-hand column is a rerun, not new work.
Heads up for Stem 6 (surfaces) The kill list on the left carries over unchanged — same four items, same criterion. The right-hand column doesn't: a surface has no single genus/degree formula forcing H¹ to vanish, so the curve's free ride off Riemann–Roch's non-special range won't repeat there. Expect one genuinely new nonskippable ingredient at Stem 6 — a vanishing theorem (Serre vanishing, or Kodaira vanishing) — to close the surface embedding argument.
