Serre duality's nondegeneracy — black box #2

⟦NEW⟧ module reservedHartshorne III.7 (algebraic) · Hodge star (analytic)
Skim layer — recognition, not understanding Session 1 built the pairing itself, honestly, on the board. What remains cited — the actual black box — is one word: perfect.

What this is — and what exactly is still owed

Be precise about what's black and what isn't. Session 1 §3 constructed the pairing $H^1(X,\mathcal O(D)) \times H^0(X,\mathcal O(K-D)) \to \mathbb C$ concretely: a class in $H^1$ is Mittag-Leffler data (principal parts), pair it against a differential by multiplying and summing residues, and well-definedness was derived — it's the residue theorem, proved by Stokes on the board. What was not derived is nondegeneracy: that this pairing detects everything — no nonzero class pairs to zero with all partners. That single word carries $h^1(D) = \ell(K-D)$, hence Riemann–Roch's correction term, hence most of session 1.

Why nondegeneracy is the real content: well-definedness says the pairing makes sense; perfectness says $H^1$ has no ghost classes — everything invisible to differentials is actually realizable Mittag-Leffler data. The sharp criterion of §4 (residues against all $\omega\in H^0(K)$ vanish $\iff$ realizable) is exactly perfectness, used.

Where it sits

Underneath everything: Riemann–Roch (both session-1 derivations invoke it), and — on the surface floor — V.1's intersection pairing construction. Its two unlock routes are the project's two tracks in miniature: algebraic = Hartshorne III.7 (dualizing sheaf, residue/trace map, $\operatorname{Ext}$'s); analytic = the Hodge theorem (duality via the Hodge star on harmonic forms, where perfectness becomes $\langle\alpha, *\bar\alpha\rangle = \|\alpha\|^2 \ne 0$ — visibly nondegenerate).

The delta

The analytic route makes nondegeneracy nearly free once harmonic representatives exist — pairing a harmonic form against its own conjugate-star gives its norm. So the entire difficulty migrates into the Hodge theorem (which is why these two black boxes are one story). The algebraic route keeps the difficulty local and explicit: the residue map $H^1(X, K_X) \to \mathbb C$ must be shown to be an isomorphism, and the pairing's perfectness is checked by a duality induction. Two genuinely different distributions of the same total labor — a clean test case for the "same proof or different proofs?" question posed at the hinge.

Key statement

$X$ smooth projective curve, $K$ canonical. The residue pairing $$H^1(X,\mathcal O(D)) \times H^0(X,\mathcal O(K-D)) \to H^1(X,K_X) \xrightarrow{\ \mathrm{res}\ } \mathbb C$$ is perfect: it induces $H^1(X,\mathcal O(D)) \cong H^0(X,\mathcal O(K-D))^*$. (Surface case, used by V.1: the same with $\omega_X$ and $H^2$.)
