Divisors (Hartshorne II.6)

Skim layer — recognition, not understanding II.7's note imports this section's notation without deriving it — this page is the decoder ring for that import.

What this is

The scheme-theoretic home of the bookkeeping you already use fluently on curves. A Weil divisor is a formal sum $D = \sum n_Y Y$ of codimension-one subvarieties — on a curve, points; on a surface, curves. A Cartier divisor is the same idea encoded by where local functions vanish: a global section of $\mathscr K^*/\mathscr O^*$, i.e. compatible local fractions modulo units. $D \sim D'$ (linear equivalence) means the difference is $\operatorname{div}(f)$ for a global rational $f$.

Session 1 §2 built all of this on a curve, by hand: divisors as zero/pole data, $\mathcal O(D)$ as "functions with poles no worse than $D$," the transition-function construction, and the punchline that $\mathcal O(D)$ depends only on the class of $D$. II.6 is that story with "point" replaced by "codimension-one subvariety" and Riemann surfaces replaced by schemes.

Where it sits

Strict prerequisite of II.7 (which quotes it as "Setup, cited not re-derived") — that's why it's the one algebraic-side box in the graph that sits before the II.7 machine. It's also flagged in II.7's own frontmatter as a ⟦NEW⟧ dependency, so it's a known debt, not an oversight.

The delta

Two things are genuinely new relative to the curve story. First, Weil vs. Cartier is a real distinction: on singular schemes they differ, and Hartshorne is careful about which is which. On the nonsingular varieties this project lives on they coincide (6.11), and the map $D \mapsto \mathscr L(D)$ identifies divisor classes with line bundles: $\operatorname{Cl}(X) \cong \operatorname{Pic}(X)$ (6.15/6.16). Second, on a surface a divisor is a curve, so "linear equivalence of divisors" becomes the equivalence under which intersection numbers are invariant — the fact V.1's axioms will demand.

Key statement

$X$ nonsingular over $k=\bar k$: Weil = Cartier, and $$\operatorname{Cl}(X) \;\cong\; \operatorname{Pic}(X), \qquad D \longmapsto \mathscr L(D),$$ with $\mathscr L(D)\otimes\mathscr L(D') = \mathscr L(D+D')$. Divisor arithmetic is line-bundle arithmetic.
