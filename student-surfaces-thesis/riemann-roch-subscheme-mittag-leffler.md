# Riemann–Roch via the subscheme structure of $D$, and a precise Mittag-Leffler criterion

A self-contained note. It reproves Riemann–Roch by a route that never inducts point-by-point, and along the way turns the vague slogan "Mittag-Leffler is a cohomology-vanishing question" into an actual checkable criterion.

## 0. Setup

$X$ is a compact Riemann surface of genus $g$, $K(X)$ its field of meromorphic functions. A **divisor** is a finite formal sum $D=\sum_P n_P\cdot P$, $n_P\in\mathbb{Z}$. The sheaf $\mathcal{O}(D)$ is defined directly, no line-bundle machinery required:
$$\mathcal{O}(D)(U) := \{f\in K(X)^*: \operatorname{div}(f)|_U\ge -D|_U\}\cup\{0\}.$$

> [!example] Anchor
> $X=\mathbb{P}^1$, $D=n\cdot[\infty]$: $H^0(X,\mathcal{O}(D))$ = polynomials of degree $\le n$, dimension $n+1$.

Write $\ell(D):=\dim_{\mathbb{C}}H^0(X,\mathcal{O}(D))$, $h^1(D):=\dim_{\mathbb{C}}H^1(X,\mathcal{O}(D))$, $K$ a canonical divisor ($\deg K = 2g-2$, $\ell(K)=g$).

> [!theorem] Serre duality
> There is a **perfect** pairing
> $$H^1(X,\mathcal{O}(D))\times H^0(X,\mathcal{O}(K-D)) \longrightarrow \mathbb{C}, \qquad (\xi,\omega)\mapsto \operatorname{res}(\xi\cdot\omega).$$
> "Perfect" means the induced map $H^0(X,\mathcal{O}(K-D)) \to H^1(X,\mathcal{O}(D))^*$ is an isomorphism — i.e. $H^0(\mathcal O(K-D))$ **is**, canonically, the dual space of $H^1(\mathcal O(D))$, not just abstractly isomorphic to it. Special case $D=0$: $H^1(\mathcal{O}_X)\cong H^0(X,K)^*$, dimension $g$.

## 1. Why $-\otimes\mathcal{O}(D)$ is an exact functor

Needed below, so settled first, cleanly.

> [!important] One-line homological-algebra proof
> $\mathcal{O}(D)$ is locally free of rank $1$, hence locally isomorphic to $\mathcal{O}_X$. Over $\mathcal{O}_X$ itself, $\operatorname{Tor}_i(-,\mathcal{O}_X)=0$ for $i>0$ — trivially, since $\mathcal{O}_X$ is free (hence projective) over itself, so no resolution is even needed: $\mathcal{O}_X\otimes M = M$, tautologically exact. $\operatorname{Tor}$ is computable locally (it commutes with restriction to opens), and $\mathcal{O}(D)$ is locally $\cong\mathcal{O}_X$, so $\operatorname{Tor}_i(-,\mathcal{O}(D))=0$ for $i>0$ everywhere on $X$. But $\operatorname{Tor}_{>0}(-,L)=0$ for all sheaves is *exactly* the statement that $-\otimes L$ is an exact functor (the standard characterization of flatness). Done.

(Equivalently, without naming Tor: exactness of sheaves is checkable stalk-by-stalk / on a trivializing cover, and on such a cover $-\otimes\mathcal{O}(D)$ is literally the identity functor. Same content, no derived-functor language.)

## 2. Riemann–Roch via the subscheme structure of $D$

Assume $D\ge0$ (effective) — see the remark at the end for general $D$.

### 2.1 $D$ as a closed subscheme

$D$ effective $\Rightarrow$ $D$ is literally a closed subscheme of $X$: a finite, $0$-dimensional scheme of length $\deg D$ (points with multiplicity). Its ideal sheaf is $\mathcal{O}(-D)$ — near a point with coefficient $n_P$, generated by $z^{n_P}$ in a local coordinate, i.e. "vanish to order $\ge n_P$." So:
$$0 \to \mathcal{O}(-D) \to \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{O}_D \to 0, \qquad \mathcal{O}_D := \mathcal{O}_X/\mathcal{O}(-D).$$

### 2.2 Tensor by $\mathcal{O}(D)$

By §1, this stays exact:
$$0 \to \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{O}(D) \to \mathcal{O}(D)|_D \to 0$$
using $\mathcal{O}(-D)\otimes\mathcal{O}(D)=\mathcal{O}_X$, and $\mathcal{O}_D\otimes\mathcal{O}(D)=\mathcal{O}(D)|_D$ (the line bundle restricted to the subscheme $D$ — this recovers the quotient sheaf $\mathcal{O}(D)/\mathcal{O}_X$ from more elementary constructions, now reached structurally).

### 2.3 The long exact sequence, truncated

$D$ is $0$-dimensional, so any coherent sheaf on it has no higher cohomology: $H^1(\mathcal{O}(D)|_D)=0$. So:
$$0\to\underbrace{H^0(\mathcal{O}_X)}_{\mathbb{C}}\to H^0(\mathcal{O}(D))\to H^0(\mathcal{O}(D)|_D)\xrightarrow{\ \delta\ } H^1(\mathcal{O}_X)\to H^1(\mathcal{O}(D))\to0.$$

### 2.4 Dimension count $\Rightarrow$ Riemann–Roch

$\dim H^0(\mathcal{O}(D)|_D)=\deg D$ (the length of the subscheme). The alternating sum of dimensions in *any* exact sequence of finite-dimensional vector spaces is $0$ — no need to chase $\ker\delta$ or $\operatorname{im}\delta$ individually:
$$1-\ell(D)+\deg D-g+h^1(D)=0$$

> [!theorem] Riemann–Roch
> $$\ell(D)-h^1(D) = \deg D + 1 - g.$$

Derived without induction — the whole divisor handled at once, via its subscheme structure.

## 3. A precise Mittag-Leffler criterion

### 3.1 The question

An element $p\in H^0(\mathcal{O}(D)|_D)$ is a choice of principal part (Laurent tail, bounded by $D$'s orders) at each point of $\operatorname{supp}(D)$. **Question:** is $p$ realized by an actual global $f\in H^0(\mathcal{O}(D))$? By exactness of §2.3, this holds **iff $\delta(p)=0$.** The rest of this section unpacks what that means, concretely.

### 3.2 Dualizing $\delta$, one step at a time

**Step 1 — pure linear algebra, nothing about curves yet.** Any linear map $\delta:V\to W$ between finite-dimensional spaces repackages, with no loss of information, as a pairing:
$$V\times W^*\to\mathbb{C}, \qquad (v,\xi)\mapsto \xi(\delta(v)).$$
Knowing this pairing for every $\xi\in W^*$ recovers $\delta(v)$ completely, since $W^{**}=W$.

**Step 2 — Serre duality tells you concretely what $W^*$ is.** Here $W=H^1(\mathcal{O}_X)$, and Serre duality ($D=0$ case, §0) says $H^0(X,K)\to H^1(\mathcal{O}_X)^*$, $\omega\mapsto(\eta\mapsto\operatorname{res}(\eta\cdot\omega))$, is an isomorphism. Substitute this concrete description of $W^*$ into Step 1:
$$V\times H^0(X,K)\to\mathbb{C}, \qquad (v,\omega)\mapsto \operatorname{res}(\delta(v)\cdot\omega).$$
Progress, but $\delta(v)$ is still an unopened black box.

**Step 3 — what $\delta(p)$ actually is, as an explicit Čech cocycle.** This is the connecting homomorphism (snake lemma), and it has a fully concrete recipe. Cover $X$ by $U_0:=X\setminus\operatorname{supp}(D)$ and small disks $U_i$ around each $P_i\in\operatorname{supp}(D)$. Given $p$, lift it to an actual local meromorphic function $\tilde p_i$ on $U_i$ with exactly that principal part (trivial — write the Laurent polynomial with the prescribed negative-order terms in a local coordinate). On the overlap $U_i\cap U_0$ (which excludes $P_i$ itself), $\tilde p_i$ has no pole left — it's holomorphic there. The collection $\{\tilde p_i\}$, read as data on these overlaps, *is* a Čech $1$-cocycle for $\mathcal{O}_X$, representing $\delta(p)$.

**Step 4 — pair that explicit cocycle against $\omega$, reusing the residue pairing (not a new rule).** Multiply the local data by $\omega$ and sum residues:
$$\operatorname{res}(\delta(p)\cdot\omega) = \sum_i \operatorname{Res}_{P_i}(\tilde p_i\cdot\omega).$$
Independent of the choice of lift: two lifts of the same $p$ differ by something holomorphic near $P_i$ (no negative-order terms), and a holomorphic function times $\omega$ has residue $0$.

### 3.3 The criterion

> [!theorem] Sharp Mittag-Leffler criterion
> $$p \text{ is realizable} \iff \sum_{P\in\operatorname{supp}(D)}\operatorname{Res}_P(p\cdot\omega) = 0 \quad\text{for every } \omega\in H^0(X,K).$$

Not a blanket statement about $D$ — a checkable condition on the *specific* data $p$.

### 3.4 Worked example: a single simple pole

$D=P$, one point, coefficient $1$. Then $H^0(\mathcal{O}(D)|_D)=\mathbb{C}$: a single number $c$, meaning "prescribe $f=c/z+O(1)$ near $P$." Lift: $\tilde p=c/z$. Pair against $\omega=h(z)\,dz$ ($h$ holomorphic near $P$):
$$\operatorname{Res}_P\!\left(\frac{c}{z}\,h(z)\,dz\right) = c\cdot h(0).$$

So this pole data is realizable iff $c\cdot h(0)=0$ for *every* holomorphic differential $\omega=h\,dz$ — i.e. (for $c\ne0$) iff every holomorphic differential vanishes at $P$. For $g\ge1$ and generic $P$, that's false — some differential doesn't vanish there — so $\delta(p)\ne0$:

> [!example] Punchline
> A meromorphic function with **exactly one simple pole** is generically not realizable on a curve of genus $\ge1$. Matches the familiar fact directly: such a function would give a degree-$1$ map to $\mathbb{P}^1$, forcing $g=0$.

## 4. The precise relationship to $H^1(D)=0$

§3.3 gives a criterion for one *specific* $p$. This section asks the global question: what does $H^1(D)=0$ say about *all* $p$ at once — and resolves an imprecision from earlier in this project (the loose claim "$H^1(D)=0$ is the Mittag-Leffler question").

### 4.1 Dualize the tail of the sequence, not just $\delta$ itself

From §2.3, the sequence ends $V\xrightarrow{\delta}W\xrightarrow{\alpha}H^1(D)\to0$ (with $V=H^0(\mathcal{O}(D)|_D)$, $W=H^1(\mathcal{O}_X)$), and $\alpha$ is automatically surjective. A linear map is surjective iff its dual is injective — but more simply here, since $\alpha$'s target is what we're asking about:
$$H^1(D)=0 \iff \alpha \text{ is the zero map} \iff \ker(\alpha)=W \iff \operatorname{im}(\delta)=W \iff \delta \text{ is surjective}.$$

### 4.2 Translate "$\delta$ surjective" through the residue pairing

$\delta$ surjective $\iff$ its transpose $\delta^t:H^0(X,K)\to V^*$ is injective. Concretely (§3.2's construction), $\delta^t(\omega)$ is the functional $p\mapsto\sum_P\operatorname{Res}_P(p\cdot\omega)$. So:
$$\delta^t \text{ injective} \iff \Big[\textstyle\sum_P\operatorname{Res}_P(p\cdot\omega)=0\ \forall p \;\Rightarrow\; \omega=0\Big].$$

### 4.3 Identify $\ker(\delta^t)$ outright: it's $H^0(X,K-D)$

If $\omega$ vanishes to order $\ge n_P$ at every point of $D$'s support — i.e. $\omega\in H^0(K-D)$ — then multiplying by *any* Laurent tail $p$ with pole order $\le n_P$ leaves no pole, so the residue vanishes automatically, for every $p$. That's containment one way. A dimension count closes it: $\dim\ker(\delta^t)=\dim W - \operatorname{rank}(\delta)=g-(g-h^1(D))=h^1(D)$ (using §4.1's surjectivity relation and Riemann–Roch), matching $\dim H^0(K-D)=\ell(K-D)=h^1(D)$ exactly (Serre duality). Equal dimension plus containment $\Rightarrow$ equality.

### 4.4 The clean statement

> [!theorem] What $H^1(D)=0$ actually means
> $$H^1(D)=0 \iff H^0(K-D)=0 \iff \text{every } p\in H^0(\mathcal{O}(D)|_D) \text{ is realizable, with no exceptions.}$$

Contrast with the loose earlier claim: $H^1(D)=0$ is **not** about whether *some* nonzero $f\in H^0(D)$ exists (§3.4 already showed that question can be trivially yes or no for reasons unrelated to $h^1$). It's about whether **every conceivable prescribed pole data bounded by $D$** is realizable — §3.3's criterion becomes vacuously true for all $p$ exactly when no nonzero holomorphic differential is left that could ever detect an obstruction. That's the honest content of "Mittag-Leffler holds unconditionally for this $D$."

> [!example] Sanity check — genus $0$
> $X=\mathbb{P}^1$: $H^0(K)=0$ outright ($\deg K=-2$), so $H^1(\mathcal{O}_X)=0$, forcing $H^1(D)=0$ for *every* divisor $D$ — matching "Mittag-Leffler is unconditional on $\mathbb{P}^1$," for the cleanest possible reason: there's nothing left to obstruct with.

---

## 5. Remark: general (not-necessarily-effective) $D$

Everything above used $D\ge0$ to get a genuine closed subscheme. For general $D$, either write $D=D_1-D_2$ with $D_1,D_2\ge0$ and chain the argument, or use the alternative point-by-point induction (compare $D$ and $D-P$ via $0\to\mathcal{O}(D-P)\to\mathcal{O}(D)\to k(P)\to0$, one point at a time) — same conclusion, different route, not reproduced here.
