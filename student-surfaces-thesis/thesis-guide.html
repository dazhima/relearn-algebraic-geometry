From Black Boxes to a Thesis

A march through Hartshorne, Chapters IV and V, aimed at an undergraduate thesis and at building the one thing more theorems won't give you: mileage on real surfaces.
in progress planned / not started
Update this diagram by hand as sessions happen — it's meant to be a living status page, not a fixed plan.

This page is not a substitute for Hartshorne's Algebraic Geometry (GTM 52) — it's the map. Reading Chapters II and III start to finish before reaching a single surface would spend most of a year proving lemmas whose payoff isn't yet visible. This guide takes a deliberately narrow slice of Chapter IV (curves) — just Riemann–Roch and Hurwitz — and then goes straight into Chapter V (surfaces), the actual center of gravity. Only after surfaces are standing on their own, and after the Hodge-theory/cohomology unlock, does the guide loop back to the rest of Chapter IV: embeddings, elliptic curves, the canonical curve. Wherever an argument needs a theorem from Chapter II or III that hasn't been built yet, this guide names it, marks it a black box, and keeps moving. The black boxes don't disappear — they get logged, and once surfaces are built we go back and open the load-bearing ones from two independent directions at once.

Where you stand

You arrive at this book unusually well prepared — just not in the way most people expect "prepared for algebraic geometry" to look. You already know affine schemes, and you've read four books that, on the surface, look unrelated to Hartshorne but are quietly exactly what its Chapter III needs, dressed in different clothes.

None of that is a coincidence. do Carmo and Bott–Tu are, in disguise, the analytic side of exactly the cohomological machinery Hartshorne develops algebraically in Chapter III. That correspondence is the whole premise of the two-route strategy described below.

The actual gap It isn't more theorems — it's research obstruction: the experience of deciding what's true about an object by playing with it, rather than by looking up the theorem that settles it. Nothing on the reading list above trains that. Living inside two chapters of concrete geometry does.

The bet: geometry before machinery

Chapter IV covers divisors on curves, the Riemann–Roch theorem, the Hurwitz formula for ramified covers, embeddings into projective space, elliptic curves, and the classification of curves in 𝔽³. Chapter V covers intersection theory on a surface, ruled surfaces, blow-ups (Hartshorne calls them "monoidal transformations"), the beginning of the classification of surfaces, birational transformations, and classical examples — cubic surfaces, del Pezzo surfaces.

Read straight through Chapters II and III first, and a full year can go by before a single interesting surface appears. Read IV and V first — treating II and III as reference material, consulted only when a specific black box demands it — and week one already has a real object on the table.

The risk is real and worth naming honestly: both chapters lean on Chapter III constantly. Riemann–Roch for curves is proved using Serre duality and the cohomology of line bundles; the existence of the intersection pairing on a surface, and Riemann–Roch for surfaces, need the same machinery plus intersection theory. You cannot read IV–V as fully self-contained. What you can do is use the theorems as black boxes on first contact, and go back for the proofs once you know why they matter — which is the entire point of the method below.

The method: black-box-first

The good news is that this is not a shortcut invented for this guide — it's how Hartshorne's own Chapter V is written.

Theorem (Hartshorne V.1) — existence and uniqueness of the intersection pairing

Let 𝕏 be a nonsingular projective surface over an algebraically closed field. There is a unique pairing Div(𝕏) × Div(𝕏) → ℤ, written (C, D) ↦ C·D, such that:

  • if C and D are nonsingular curves meeting transversally, C·D equals the number of points in C ∩ D;
  • it is symmetric: C·D = D·C;
  • it is additive in each argument;
  • it depends only on the linear equivalence classes of C and D.

The construction of this pairing — the part that actually needs cohomology — comes after the theorem is stated. On a first pass, you use the four properties exactly as Hartshorne's own reader does.

Worked example — why the axioms alone are already enough

Let L ⊂ 𝔽² be a line. What is L·L? You can't count points of L ∩ L directly — that's not a transverse intersection. Instead, pick a different line L′ linearly equivalent to L (any two lines in 𝔽² are linearly equivalent, and two distinct lines meet transversally in exactly one point). Then $$L\cdot L = L\cdot L' \quad(\text{linear-equivalence invariance})\quad = \#(L\cap L') = 1.$$ One line of reasoning, no cohomology, and it already tells you self-intersection is a real, computable invariant.

Every time an argument needs something not yet built — Serre duality, the existence of this very pairing, the fact that cohomology of a coherent sheaf on a projective variety is finite-dimensional — name it, log it, and move on. A running log needs only three columns:

Two routes, one comparison

The thesis is not "read Hartshorne." It's the comparison of two independent proofs of the same black box — because you two happen to be equipped for exactly the two sides, and each side now has an actual book behind it.

Main target Not "cover more of Hartshorne." The target is the short list of black boxes that IV and V keep invoking — Serre duality, Riemann–Roch's own proof, the existence of the intersection pairing, adjunction. Both routes below point at exactly that list, from opposite directions.

Student — algebraic route

Primary source: Hartshorne, Chapter III — the very chapter this plan skips on first pass, read now with a reason to want each theorem.

  • Čech cohomology of sheaves on a Noetherian space
  • Cohomology of 𝒪(d) on 𝔽n by explicit complex
  • Serre duality via the residue/trace map
  • Riemann–Roch derived from Euler characteristic + Serre duality
  • Intersection numbers via Fulton's deformation to the normal cone

Yan — analytic / Hodge route

Primary source: Huybrechts, Complex Geometry: An Introduction — the appendix on harmonic theory (Riemannian Hodge theorem), then §3.2 "Hodge Theory on Kähler Manifolds," picking up exactly where his reading stopped.

  • Dolbeault cohomology Hp,q∂̄
  • Harmonic representatives via Hodge theory
  • Serre duality as Hodge-star / ∂̄-Poincaré duality
  • Riemann–Roch on a Riemann surface via the Hodge decomposition
  • Adjunction and self-intersection via Chern–Weil curvature

It runs in both directions: the student leads because homological algebra and spectral sequences (Bott–Tu) are already close to fluent for him, while Hodge theory is a gap for him — and, honestly, for Yan too, so Huybrechts's appendix is the shared starting point rather than a lecture one-way. Teaching happens both ways: two books, two proofs, one shared target.

M2 — Chapter IV fast pass: curves planned

Riemann–Roch is used immediately, not proved:

Theorem (Riemann–Roch for curves)

For a divisor D on a smooth projective curve of genus g, with canonical divisor K and ℓ(D) := dim H⁰(X, 𝒪(D)): $$\ell(D) - \ell(K-D) = \deg D + 1 - g.$$

Two immediate uses: on 𝔽¹ (g = 0), this recovers h⁰(𝒪(d)) = d+1 for d \ge 0 almost for free. On an elliptic curve (g = 1, K = 0), it collapses to ℓ(D) = \deg D for any effective D ≠ 0 — the fact used to embed elliptic curves in projective space. The gap between a naive dimension count and the Riemann–Roch answer is the first real black box: it's exactly Serre duality's correction term ℓ(K-D).

Degree–genus formula, via adjunction

For a smooth plane curve C of degree d, adjunction gives K_C = (K_{\mathbb P^2} + C)|_C = \mathcal O(d-3)|_C, so \deg K_C = d(d-3). Since \deg K_C = 2g-2:

$$g = \frac{(d-1)(d-2)}{2}.$$

The Hurwitz formula extends the same idea to ramified covers f: C \to C' of degree n: $$2g_C - 2 = n(2g_{C'}-2) + \sum_P (e_P - 1).$$ Elliptic curves are also the natural bridge onward — moduli of curves, arithmetic geometry — the material a master's-level course builds on next.

M3 — Chapter V fast pass: surfaces planned

This is the actual center of gravity: the objects that make surfaces feel real, and worth a thesis.

Blow-ups. Blowing up a point on a smooth surface X produces \pi: \widetilde X \to X with exceptional divisor E ≅ 𝔽¹, satisfying E^2 = -1 and $$K_{\widetilde X} = \pi^*K_X + E.$$ The algebraic construction is already on file as the deformation-to-normal-cone brick; what's new here is the picture do Carmo never gave you — what curvature concentrates on when you blow up a point.

Castelnuovo's criterion. Any curve E ⊂ X with E \cong \mathbb P^1 and E^2=-1 — a "(−1)-curve" — is exactly the exceptional divisor of some blow-down. This is the tool used to find minimal models: every smooth projective surface is a finite sequence of blow-ups of a surface with no (−1)-curves, and minimal models are classified by Kodaira dimension — the Enriques–Kodaira classification, the natural horizon just past this thesis.

A bridge worth pausing on — Noether's formula For a smooth projective surface, $$\chi(\mathcal O_X) = \tfrac{1}{12}\big(K_X^2 + \chi_{\text{top}}(X)\big).$$ The left side is algebraic (an Euler characteristic of a sheaf); the right side mixes an intersection number with the topological Euler characteristic — exactly the kind of algebra/topology bridge Bott–Tu already primed you to expect.

Cubic surfaces and the 27 lines. See the anchor section below for the full picture — it's rich enough to carry a thesis by itself.

Thesis anchors — three live options

Any of these is a legitimate undergraduate thesis. The choice is his, made together after M3 — not tomorrow.

Cubic surfaces & the 27 lines

A smooth cubic surface X \subset \mathbb P^3 is isomorphic to \mathbb P^2 blown up at six points in general position (no three collinear, not all six on a conic). Its Picard group has rank 7, with basis H, E_1,\dots,E_6 (H the pullback of a line, E_i the exceptional divisors), and intersection form $$H^2=1,\quad E_i^2=-1,\quad H\cdot E_i = 0,\quad E_i\cdot E_j = 0\ (i\neq j).$$ The 27 lines split into three families of classes: the six E_i themselves; the fifteen classes H - E_i - E_j (strict transforms of lines through two of the six points); and the six classes 2H - E_1-\cdots-E_6+E_i (strict transforms of conics through five of the six points, omitting one). 6+15+6=27.

Why it's more than a computation The incidence pattern of the 27 lines has a symmetry group equal to the Weyl group W(E_6), of order 51840 — acting on \mathrm{Pic}(X) as exactly the orthogonal group preserving the intersection form on K_X^{\perp}, which is the E_6 root lattice. A 19th-century enumerative-geometry fact turns out to encode one of the exceptional Lie groups. Self-contained, classical, and strong enough to anchor a real thesis on its own.

Hirzebruch / ruled surfaces

The Hirzebruch surface F_n = \mathbb P(\mathcal O_{\mathbb P^1} \oplus \mathcal O_{\mathbb P^1}(n)) \to \mathbb P^1 is a \mathbb P^1-bundle carrying a distinguished section of self-intersection -n; together with F_0 = \mathbb P^1\times\mathbb P^1, these are exactly the ruled surfaces over \mathbb P^1. This anchor connects directly to do Carmo's curvature background and to two bricks already on file — relative-canonical-bundle and direct-image — since a ruled surface is literally the projectivization of a rank-2 pushforward.

Blow-ups & minimal models

The broadest option: build up the machinery of (−1)-curves and Castelnuovo's criterion toward the classification of minimal surfaces by Kodaira dimension. Most "research obstruction" flavored of the three, and the longest runway — best suited if there's more than one semester to work with.

Session 1 agenda — tomorrow, 2026-07-02

Six movements: state the two theorems, walk the corollaries up to the surprising one, take one glimpse further out than the thesis actually needs, reveal that everything just used was a black box, then zoom out to the attack plan and the big picture. Content in the order to present it.

1. Riemann–Roch and Serre duality, stated (not proved)

Riemann–Roch (curves)

For a divisor D on a smooth projective curve X of genus g, with canonical divisor K and \ell(D) := \dim H^0(X,\mathcal O(D)): $$\ell(D) - \ell(K-D) = \deg D + 1 - g.$$

Serre duality (curves)

$$h^1(X,\mathcal O(D)) = \ell(K-D).$$ So the "correction term" in Riemann–Roch is not a fudge factor — it's the dimension of another, dual space of sections. Riemann–Roch and Serre duality are really one package: dimension count in, dimension count out.

2. Five corollaries — walk them in this order

  1. Genus is a space of differentials. Set D=0: \ell(\mathcal O)=1 (only constants are global functions on a projective curve), so 1-\ell(K)=1-g, giving $$\ell(K) = g.$$ The genus — a priori just a number from topology or a degree-2 formula — turns out to literally count independent holomorphic 1-forms. This is the fact do Carmo and Bott–Tu already have the vocabulary for: a holomorphic differential is a very concrete object.
  2. Above a threshold, the correction term vanishes. If \deg D > 2g-2, then \deg(K-D)<0, so \ell(K-D)=0 (a divisor of negative degree has no sections), leaving the exact formula $$\ell(D) = \deg D + 1 - g.$$ Serre duality's correction term only ever matters in the finite window 0 \le \deg D \le 2g-2 — call divisors outside that window non-special. This is the cleanest possible answer to "why do we even need Serre duality:" it's a bounded-size patch, not a permanent tax.
  3. Riemann–Roch is an embedding machine. If \deg D \ge 2g+1, the linear system |D| is very ample and embeds X \hookrightarrow \mathbb P^{\deg D - g}. Dimension-counting turns into "here is a projective space your curve sits inside, and here is exactly which one."
  4. Elliptic curves are cubics. g=1 \Rightarrow K=0 (an elliptic curve carries a nowhere-vanishing holomorphic 1-form — itself worth pausing on). Take D=3P for a point P: \deg D = 3 > 2g-2=0, so \ell(D)=3+1-1=3, embedding the curve in \mathbb P^2 as the familiar Weierstrass cubic.
  5. The hook: every genus-2 curve is hyperelliptic. For g=2: \ell(K)=2, \deg K = 2. The canonical map \varphi_K lands in \mathbb P^{1} with degree 2 — so every genus-2 curve is a double cover of \mathbb P^1, branched at 6 points (Riemann–Hurwitz: 2(2)-2 = 2(2(0)-2)+\#\text{branch pts}). This is not true for most curves of genus \ge 3 — genus 2 is forced into it. A three-line argument from a theorem stated without proof produces a fact that's genuinely surprising.

3. A glimpse further out — Nevanlinna theory

Riemann–Hurwitz, just used above, is a statement about finite, honest covers of compact Riemann surfaces — completely algebraic. It has a transcendental twin worth showing him precisely because it isn't needed for the thesis: it's here to demonstrate that the same invariant governs a totally different world.

Nevanlinna's Second Main Theorem

For a nonconstant meromorphic function f:\mathbb C \to \mathbb P^1 and distinct points a_1,\dots,a_q \in \mathbb P^1: $$(q-2)\,T(r,f) \;\le\; \sum_{j=1}^{q} \bar N(r,a_j) \;+\; S(r,f),$$ where T(r,f) is the Nevanlinna characteristic (growth rate, standing in for "degree" now that \mathbb C isn't compact so there's no honest degree to count), \bar N(r,a_j) counts a_j-points without multiplicity, and S(r,f) is genuinely small.

The constant 2 here is not a coincidence — it's \chi(\mathbb P^1)=2, the same 2 sitting inside "2g-2" for g=0 in the Riemann–Hurwitz formula above. Two unrelated-looking worlds — finite algebraic covers, and entire transcendental maps from \mathbb C — and the identical invariant falls out of both.

The corollary he already half-knows: Picard's little theorem — a nonconstant entire function omits at most one finite value — is exactly the q=3 case of the defect relation implied by the Second Main Theorem. A first-year complex analysis fact turns out to be a shadow of the same formula governing ramified covers in algebraic geometry. (One further step, worth naming but not developing: Vojta's dictionary carries this same "2" into Diophantine approximation and arithmetic geometry — heights, effective finiteness theorems — so this one constant sits at a genuine crossroads of complex analysis, algebraic geometry, and number theory.)

4. The reveal

Everything above used two theorems that were never proved — just stated and trusted. That's the method, made visible in real time: name the black box, log it, keep the geometry moving. Open the black-box log together and enter the first two rows: Riemann–Roch, Serre duality — statement used, where it mattered (five times already, in one sitting), route to unlock later (see below).

5. What we attack next, and the big picture

This is the actual pitch, in three parts:

  1. A real math result, done live. Everything in parts 1–3 above — five corollaries and a transcendental cousin, out of two unproved theorems. That already happened; point back at it.
  2. An analytic approach and an algebraic approach to the fundamentals. Two independent books, two independent proofs of the same short list of theorems — see the attack plan: he works from Hartshorne III (Čech cohomology, the residue pairing, Riemann–Roch from Euler characteristics), Yan works from Huybrechts (harmonic theory, then §3.2 on Kähler manifolds — Dolbeault, Hodge decomposition, Serre duality as Hodge duality). Neither route is "the real one" — they're checked against each other.
  3. The target is named, not open-ended. Not "read more of Hartshorne" — the target is the specific short list of black boxes that Chapters IV and V keep invoking: Serre duality, Riemann–Roch's own proof, existence of the intersection pairing, adjunction. See the module map and thesis anchors — the same black-box-first move that just worked for curves (Chapter IV) repeats for surfaces (Chapter V), and lands on a chosen concrete surface as an actual thesis.

Why this is promising

Done well, this thesis sits right at the entrance to first-year master's algebraic geometry — that's the point of building it this way rather than as a survey of Hartshorne.

It produces a real deliverable, not reading notes: a cross-checked, two-proof understanding of the theorems behind one concrete surface. It uses what he already has instead of asking for more of it — do Carmo and Bott–Tu stop being background he once read and become the analytic lens he applies himself, under guidance. And it leaves an obvious next step already visible on the map: classification of surfaces, moduli of curves, K3 surfaces, or the transcendental side glimpsed via Nevanlinna theory and hyperbolicity — depending on which direction his master's study takes. Whichever it is, this thesis is the on-ramp, not a detour before the real work starts.
